Lesson 1.2: Cell Interactions

It turns out that the cellular automaton mentioned in the previous section is using a commonly studied ruleset called rule 30. To further explore these rules, let’s go through some of the foundational parts of interaction between cells.

The first important concept is called a generation. You have actually already seen this in the previous section. A generation can be thought of as a time step in a cellular automaton with a given state. Where have we seen this before? Let’s look back at rule 30, with the first generation with a single on cell in the center:

In this example, a generation is represented by each individual row. Whenever you clicked on Generate Next Step, you were actually creating a new generation of the cellular automata! But how can you define the rules of this automaton?

Rules for a cellular automaton are created based on groups of cells called neighborhoods. A cell’s neighbors are those that are surrounding a particular cell, within a certain distance. The ruleset for rule 30 defines the neighborhood for a cell to be cells directly adjacent to the cell to the left and right. Note that we assume the left/right neighbors of the far left/right cells, respectively, are assumed to be an off or dead cell. Select a cell below to see its neighbors:

Neighborhoods can be defined in different ways and the interaction between the state of cells and their neighbors over time form the basis for the rules of a cellular automaton. Knowing this, how can we define the ruleset for rule 30? Let’s take a closer look at the first two generations of the automata above. Focus on the three living (on) cells in the center of the second generation. The state of these cells is determined by the state of their neighborhood in the previous generation (in this case generation 1).
